Tips for Financial Security

Protecting your financial information is more important than ever in today's digital world. Here are some straightforward, practical steps you can take to keep your accounts secure.

1. Think Before You Click

Cybercriminals often disguise malicious links in emails or text messages that look legitimate. If you get an unexpected message asking for personal information, don't click on anything. Instead, visit your bank's website directly or use their official app to verify the information. When in doubt, ignore or delete suspicious messages.

2. Create Strong, Unique Passwords

Passwords are like keys to your financial life, so make them strong. Use a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols, and avoid obvious choices like birthdays or simple words. Each account should have a unique password. To keep track of them all, try using a password manager—it'll do the heavy lifting for you.

3. Turn On Multifactor Authentication (MFA)

MFA adds an extra step to your login process—like entering a code sent to your phone in addition to your password. It might feel like a small hassle, but it's one of the best defenses against unauthorized access.

4. Keep Your Devices Updated

Think of software updates as essential maintenance for your cybersecurity. Make sure your phone, computer, and apps are updated regularly to patch vulnerabilities hackers might exploit. If you store sensitive information in the cloud, check that the service provider uses strong encryption and offers MFA.

5. Avoid Public Devices and Wi-Fi Networks

Public computers and open Wi-Fi networks are convenient but risky. Hackers can easily intercept information transmitted over unsecured connections. If you must use public Wi-Fi, connect through a virtual private network (VPN) to add a layer of protection.

6. Browse Safely

When logging into financial accounts, ensure the website's URL starts with "https"—the "s" stands for secure. Look for a padlock icon next to the web address. Avoid visiting other sites while you're logged in, and always log out when you're done.

7. Monitor Your Accounts Regularly

Make a habit of checking your account activity often. Many banks offer real-time alerts for transactions, logins, or changes to your account details. If something looks off, contact your bank immediately.

If You Think Your Account Has Been Compromised

Don't panic—act fast. Change your password right away and notify your bank. Consider placing a fraud alert on your credit file and checking your credit reports for unfamiliar accounts. You can also visit IdentityTheft.gov for further assistance. Staying proactive with these habits can make a big difference in keeping your financial information safe. Cybersecurity isn't just a task for your bank—it's a partnership, and your vigilance is key.
